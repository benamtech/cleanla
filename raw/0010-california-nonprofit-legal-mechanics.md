# California / LA Nonprofit Legal Mechanics for Civic Tech (2026)

> Compiled 2026-05-24 for CleanLA's attorney conversation prep. **NOT legal advice** — preparation so the attorney conversation is efficient.

## Executive Summary

**Two paths exist for CleanLA:**

1. **Direct 501(c)(3) incorporation** (3–6 months, $2,500–5,000+ attorney + filing): File Articles with CA Secretary of State, then apply to IRS. California-specific filings follow. More control; slower; highest upfront legal cost.

2. **Fiscal sponsorship** (4–8 weeks, 9–15% revenue fee ongoing): Use an established 501(c)(3) as the legal owner; CleanLA operates as a "sponsored project." No incorporation needed; faster start; lower legal cost; founder has less legal control; ongoing fee structure.

**For a 14-day attorney prep conversation**, clarify which path fits: Does the founder want to build CleanLA as an independent 501(c)(3) long-term, or test it quickly under a sponsor while building governance chops?

## Path A: Direct 501(c)(3) Incorporation

### Timeline (realistic, end-to-end)

- **State incorporation**: 1–3 weeks (CA Secretary of State processes Articles of Incorporation @ $30)
- **IRS determination letter**: 3–6 months (Form 1023, the full application) or 2–4 weeks (Form 1023-EZ, if eligible)
- **CA Attorney General registration**: 30 days from first charitable asset / donation (Form CT-1, $50 fee)
- **Grant-ready**: After IRS determination letter arrives. Some funders require 6+ months of audited operations; others grant immediately post-determination.

**Total elapsed time**: 4–7 months (state + IRS, assuming no IRS requests for additional info; can stretch if IRS has questions).

**27-month rule**: Must file Form 1023 within 27 months of incorporation or IRS exemption is effective only from filing date forward (not retroactive). This matters for early grant eligibility and donor deductions.

### Cost breakdown

- **State filing** (Articles of Incorporation): $30
- **IRS Form 1023-EZ** (if eligible): $275 (requires gross receipts < $50K projected, assets < $250K)
- **IRS Form 1023** (full application, if not eligible): $600
- **Attorney fees** (state incorporation + IRS application): $1,500–5,000 depending on complexity and whether attorney is starting from scratch vs. refining founder documents
  - Many CA nonprofits use flat-fee packages bundling bylaws, Articles, and Form 1023 prep: $2,500–5,000
  - Hourly rates: $150–400/hr for nonprofit specialists
- **CA Attorney General registration** (Form CT-1): $50
- **Annual CA filings** (Form RRF-1 + Form 990 or CT-TR-1): Free to file; CPA costs to prepare, $500–2,000/year

**Total Year 1 legal + registration**: $2,500–5,600 (attorney + filing fees). **Year 2+**: $500–2,000/yr just for annual filings.

### Board minimum

- **Minimum: 3 directors** (CA Corporations Code §5213)
- **Disinterested majority required**: No more than 49% of board can be compensated by the org or related (family/business ties)
  - Implication: Founder can serve as director + Executive Director (staff), but cannot control > 50% vote
  - Common structure: Founder as ED (non-voting); 3+ independent board members; founder may sit as director with vote if board has independent majority
- **Required officers**: President, Secretary, Treasurer at minimum
- **No one-person control**: Board must meet regularly, maintain minutes, and provide oversight (not rubber-stamp the ED)

### California-specific filings after incorporation

1. **Form CT-1** (Registry of Charities & Fundraisers, Initial Registration)
   - Due: Within 30 days of first charitable asset/donation
   - Fee: $50
   - Requires: Articles, bylaws, IRS determination letter
   - Filed with: CA Attorney General, Charities Registry

2. **Annual Form RRF-1** (Annual Registration Renewal)
   - Due: Every calendar year by June 15 (extension to August 15)
   - Paired with: Form 990 (if revenue > $500K) or Form 990-EZ (if $200K–500K) or Form 990-N e-Postcard (if < $50K)
   - Or California Form CT-TR-1 (if form 990 not required)
   - Filed with: CA Attorney General

3. **Statement of Information** (Form SI-100 or SOP-100, Secretary of State)
   - Due: Within 90 days of incorporation, then every 2 years
   - Fee: $0 if filed online; $15 if by mail
   - Requires: Current board officers, addresses, conflict of interest policy certification

4. **CA Franchise Tax Board Form 3500A** (Request for Exemption)
   - Usually automatic with IRS determination letter, but proactive filing advisable
   - No fee; exempts from state income tax
   - Some nonprofits file to ensure FTB alignment with IRS

## Path B: Fiscal Sponsorship (Faster Start)

Fiscal sponsorship means an established 501(c)(3) acts as the legal owner and operator of CleanLA. CleanLA is a "sponsored project" with no separate legal entity. The sponsor handles banking, taxes, compliance.

### Why it matters
- **Timeline**: 4–8 weeks from application to approval (vs. 4–7 months for direct incorporation)
- **Upfront cost**: No attorney needed; mostly application materials
- **Ongoing cost**: 9–15% of revenue (fee structure varies by sponsor)
- **Legal control**: Founder has less fiduciary control; sponsor holds the 501(c)(3) and board
- **Grant eligibility**: Often immediate (sponsor is already 501(c)(3)-recognized)

### Major LA-area fiscal sponsors for civic tech

#### **Community Partners (Los Angeles)**
- **Reach**: 30+ years, 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor, hundreds of sponsored projects
- **Fee structure**: 9% of private revenue + 15% of public revenue (government grants, contracts)
- **Application timeline**: 4–8 weeks typical
- **Services**: Financial management, HR, insurance, legal compliance, admin support
- **Best for**: Community-based and civic-minded projects; strong fit for CleanLA's mission profile
- **Website**: [https://communitypartners.org/fiscal-sponsorship/](https://communitypartners.org/fiscal-sponsorship/)

#### **Social Good Fund (Oakland, CA)**
- **Reach**: 725 projects as of 2024 (grew from 200 in 2018)
- **Philosophy**: "Start with a yes for any group with charitable purpose aligned with our mission"
- **Fee structure**: Not specified in available sources; typical fiscal sponsors charge 5–15% of revenue
- **Application timeline**: ~4–6 weeks (typical)
- **Best for**: Organizations seeking rapid activation and low friction
- **Website**: Social Impact Fund / Social Good Fund ecosystem

#### **Players Philanthropy Fund**
- **Reach**: Athlete-led philanthropy; helps athletes create nonprofits and community programs
- **Fee structure**: Not specified; standard is 10–12% of revenue
- **Application timeline**: Likely 4–6 weeks
- **Best for**: If CleanLA has athlete or sports-adjacent supporters
- **Note**: Mission may be narrower than civic tech; check alignment with staff training focus

#### **Hack for LA** (LA-specific civic tech brigade)
- **Status**: Code for America Brigade (volunteer-driven, not traditional fiscal sponsor)
- **Governance**: Fiscal sponsorship model is unclear; may partner with larger sponsor
- **Best for**: Civic tech network building, volunteer engagement, local government partnerships
- **Note**: Check current fiscal sponsorship availability; primarily a community organization
- **Website**: [https://www.hackforla.org/](https://www.hackforla.org/)

### Fiscal sponsorship trade-offs

| Aspect | Direct 501(c)(3) | Fiscal Sponsorship |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Timeline to grant-ready** | 4–7 months | 4–8 weeks |
| **Upfront legal cost** | $2,500–5,000+ | ~$500 (application support) |
| **Ongoing revenue cost** | Board oversight, annual filings (~$1–2K/yr) | 9–15% of all revenue |
| **Founder control** | Founder on board, shapes governance | Sponsor controls; founder is staff/director |
| **Independence** | Full legal independence | Depends on sponsor's policies |
| **Scaling to own 501(c)(3)** | N/A | Possible; transition to independent incorporation later (cost + legal work) |
| **Board requirement** | 3+ directors; founder can be one | Sponsor's board (not founder's governance) |

## Founder IP Assignment: The Mechanics

When a founder transfers code, documentation, or IP created before incorporation to the nonprofit, the transfer must be **intentional and documented** to avoid future disputes.

### Three common approaches

#### **1. Copyright assignment agreement** (most common)
- **What it does**: Founder (assignor) transfers all copyright ownership to nonprofit (assignee)
- **Covers**: Existing code, documentation, designs created pre-incorporation
- **Form**: Written agreement signed by founder, ideally witnessed or notarized
- **Tax treatment**: May qualify as charitable contribution (deductible if founder has basis); consult CPA
- **Template availability**: 
  - [LawDepot free template](https://www.lawdepot.com/us/business/assignment-of-copyright/)
  - [LegalZoom copyright assignment](https://www.legalzoom.com/templates/t/copyright-assignment)
  - [Contractbook IP assignment](https://contractbook.com/templates/intellectual-property-ip-assignment-template)
  - [Zegal founder IP assignment](https://zegal.com/founders-ip-assignment/)

#### **2. Work made for hire** (rare for pre-existing work)
- **What it does**: Legally treats work as if nonprofit created it, not founder
- **Challenge**: Work-for-hire only applies to works created *after* relationship begins; retroactive application is weak
- **Not recommended for**: Code written before founder decided to form nonprofit

#### **3. Oral agreement or acknowledgment** (NOT recommended)
- **Risk**: No documented proof; IRS may question if contribution is real
- **For nonprofits**: Always require written agreement

### Key terms in a copyright assignment

1. **Identify parties**: Full legal names, addresses (founder + nonprofit)
2. **Describe IP**: Code repos, file names, commit history, documentation, designs
3. **Statement of ownership**: Founder certifies they own it, it's unencumbered, no third-party rights
4. **Transfer language**: "Founder assigns all right, title, and interest…"
5. **Effective date**: When transfer takes effect (usually date of signature)
6. **Ongoing IP**: Clarify whether future code created by founder as ED is also assigned (likely yes)
7. **Consultation clause**: If founder continues as developer, can they use the code? (address derivative works, licensing)

### Tax implications

- **Founder's deduction**: If code has fair market value basis, founder may claim charitable contribution deduction (consult CPA re: valuation)
- **Nonprofit's basis**: Code becomes nonprofit asset; no income to nonprofit
- **No UBIT trigger**: Copyright transfer itself does not trigger unrelated business income tax

### Common mistakes

- **No written agreement**: Founder and nonprofit both assume ownership; ambiguity kills grant applications
- **Assigning code but not docs**: Transfer code but leave documentation in founder's control → nonprofit can't build on it
- **Forgetting ongoing IP**: Founder creates new features as ED; unclear if those are assigned or founder retains them
- **Not addressing open-source license**: If code is GPL-licensed, assignment doesn't change license obligations; must be transparent in assignment

## Board Governance Traps: What Goes Wrong

### Trap 1: Founder control without disinterested board
**The problem**: Founder fills board with allies, controls majority vote, treats nonprofit like personal project.
**CA law**: [CA Corporations Code §5233 et seq.](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/corporations-code/corp-sect-5230.html) requires majority disinterested directors (no compensation or family ties).
**Red flag for IRS**: Nonprofits with founder-controlled boards face increased audit scrutiny; Form 990 asks about related-party transactions.
**Precedent**: Casa Ruby (DC, 2017) — founder exercised unilateral control over millions in grants, no board meetings, no records. Nonprofit collapsed; donors sued.

### Trap 2: Founder as ED without clarity on board role
**The problem**: Founder is ED (staff, paid) and also voting board member. Conflict of interest when board votes on ED's salary or performance.
**Best practice**: 
- ED can attend board meetings and speak to agenda items
- ED is typically *non-voting* ex officio member (or not on board at all)
- Board votes on ED compensation without ED present
- If founder wants voting power, they must have true independent board majority
**CA requirement**: Form 990 Part IV requires disclosure of founder/ED conflict; underreported = audit trigger

### Trap 3: Founder wanting to "retain control" without understanding fiduciary duty
**The problem**: Founder sees 501(c)(3) as "their" organization; doesn't grasp that board now owes fiduciary duty to the *nonprofit's mission*, not to founder.
**Fiduciary duty has three parts**:
  - **Duty of care**: Board must act reasonably informed, in good faith
  - **Duty of loyalty**: Board must act in nonprofit's interest, not personal interest; no self-dealing
  - **Duty of obedience**: Board must follow bylaws, law, mission (not founder's whims if they conflict)
**Consequence of breach**: Directors can be personally liable for damages; donors can sue for mismanagement

### Trap 4: Founder salary without board approval
**The problem**: Founder decides their own ED salary; no board review.
**IRS concern**: Form 990 Part IV flags founder-set compensation as potential excess-benefit transaction.
**CA requirement**: Board (or compensation committee) must review and approve ED salary at arm's length, documented in minutes.
**What's reasonable**: CPA compares salary to market for similar role/org size; board votes to approve with ED absent.

### Trap 5: Transitioning to board chair while ED
**The problem**: Founder wants to move from ED to board chair (volunteer role) but keep influence.
**Governance issue**: Chair sets agenda, runs meetings, nominates committees; if chair is also a funder, they dominate.
**Better approach**: Founder stays ED (paid), hire independent board chair (volunteer or paid); separate power.

### Trap 6: Skipping the conflict-of-interest policy
**The problem**: No written policy; board members unclear on what counts as conflict.
**IRS requirement**: Form 990 asks if org has written conflict-of-interest policy; "no" is a red flag.
**CA law**: [CA Corporations Code §5047.5](https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/corporations-code/corp-sect-5047-5.html) requires annual conflict disclosure; written policy is best practice.
**What's in a good policy**:
  - Definition of conflict (financial interest, family ties, competing loyalties)
  - Process for disclosure (before voting)
  - Recusal rule (interested director doesn't vote or speak to issue)
  - Annual reminder to all board members
  - Board approval of conflicts raised
  - Public Counsel provides [annotated template](https://publiccounsel.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Conflict-of-Interest-Policy-Annotated-1.pdf)

## California-Specific Requirements (Beyond Federal 501(c)(3))

### 1. Registry of Charitable Trusts (Form CT-1)
- **Authority**: CA Government Code §12580 (CA Attorney General maintains registry)
- **Who files**: Every corporation, trust, or association holding property for charitable purposes
- **When**: Within 30 days of first receiving charitable assets (donation, grant, endowment)
- **Fee**: $50
- **What to include**: Articles, bylaws, IRS determination letter
- **Annual renewal**: Form RRF-1 (due June 15 or extended to Aug 15)

### 2. Annual filings (RRF-1 + Form 990 or CT-TR-1)
- **RRF-1** (Annual Registration Renewal): Required for all registered charities
  - Renewal fee: Tiered by revenue (typically $25–250)
  - Must be paired with: IRS Form 990, 990-EZ, 990-N, or CA Form CT-TR-1
- **CA Form CT-TR-1** (if Form 990 not required): For nonprofits with < $50K revenue
- **Deadline**: By June 15 (extendable to August 15)
- **Consequence of missing deadline**: Penalties; risk of revocation if pattern of non-filing

### 3. Statement of Information (Form SI-100 or SOP-100, Secretary of State)
- **Due**: Within 90 days of incorporation, then every 2 years
- **Fee**: $0 online, $15 by mail
- **What to include**: Current board officers, addresses, conflict-of-interest policy adoption certification
- **Mistake to avoid**: Filing SOP-100 (Statement of Purposes) instead of SI-100 (Statement of Info); they're different forms

### 4. California Franchise Tax Board (FTB) Form 3500A
- **Purpose**: Request exemption from CA income tax
- **Good news**: Usually automatic once IRS sends determination letter
- **Advisable action**: File proactively (Form 3500A) to ensure FTB alignment; some nonprofits skip this and get unexpected tax bills years later
- **Cost**: Free

### 5. Charitable solicitation registration
- **If CleanLA solicits donations in CA**: Register with CA Attorney General (part of CT-1 process)
- **Note**: "Solicitation" includes online fundraising, grant applications, crowdfunding
- **Renewal**: Annual RRF-1

## Software as Charitable Asset: IP, Data, Revenue, Licensing

### Open-source license compatibility
**Question**: If CleanLA is GPL-licensed, does transferring to nonprofit change the license?
- **Answer**: No. License follows the code, not the owner. GPL stays GPL regardless of who owns it.
- **Implication**: Nonprofit can't make code proprietary; must honor GPL terms.
- **Advantage for nonprofit**: GPL ensures any derivatives stay open-source; protects nonprofit's IP.
- **Attorney should confirm**: License terms and ensure nonprofit can fulfill license obligations (crediting original author, sharing derivatives).

### User data and 501(c)(3) implications
**Question**: CleanLA collects street reports (geolocation, user phone, photos). Are there nonprofit-specific CCPA/privacy limits?
- **Answer**: No nonprofit exemption from CA Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). CleanLA must:
  - Disclose data collection and use
  - Honor user rights (access, delete, opt-out)
  - Not sell data (but CA law permits "sharing" for nonprofit mission)
- **Advantage**: Nonprofit status is *not* a privacy shield; must follow same CCPA rules as for-profits
- **No HIPAA risk** unless CleanLA handles health data (likely not for street reporting)
- **Best practice**: Privacy policy clarifies data is used for city infrastructure improvement, not commercial use.

### Revenue and UBIT (Unrelated Business Income Tax)
**Question**: Can CleanLA charge users, cities, or third parties for the app or data access?

**Answer depends on the revenue model**:

| Revenue Model | UBIT Risk | Notes |
|---------------|-----------|-------|
| **Free app** | None | Pure charitable activity; no UBIT |
| **City pays for app hosting/reports** | Low/None | Furthers charitable purpose (civic infrastructure) |
| **Charge users for premium features** | Medium | Could be UBIT if services not integral to charity; attorney must review |
| **License data to for-profit (e.g., urban planners)** | Medium–High | Passive licensing (no involvement) = lower risk; active promotion = higher UBIT risk |
| **Sell anonymized data to researchers** | Low | Royalty from passive licensing typically exempt |

**Key rule**: If CleanLA's *primary activity* is software/data sales, even if to nonprofits, it may be UBIT. If CleanLA distributes free or subsidized, UBIT risk is minimal.

**Recommendation**: Have CPA review revenue model with IRS Form 990 Schedule C (Unrelated Business Income) in mind.

### Code as donated asset (valuation, basis)
**Question**: For the copyright assignment, how does founder claim a tax deduction?

**Answer**:
- **Basis**: Founder must establish "fair market value" of code (e.g., what would a developer charge to write it?)
- **Valuation methods**: 
  - Cost approach: Lines of code × $/line (e.g., $50/line in CA = 10K lines = $500K, but this is aggressive)
  - Market approach: What similar code / open-source projects are valued at (often very low without commercial traction)
  - Income approach: What revenue code might generate (risky; IRS skeptical of speculative future revenue)
- **Practical reality**: Startups usually value contributed code conservatively ($10K–50K) to avoid IRS challenge
- **CPA required**: Only a CPA or valuation expert can sign a Form 8283 (noncash charitable contribution) supporting the deduction
- **IRS scrutiny**: High-value code contributions (> $100K) trigger Form 8283-C and appraisal requirement

### Hosting costs (Vercel, Supabase) as program expense
**Question**: CleanLA's Vercel / Supabase bills are "program expense" for the nonprofit, right?
- **Answer**: Yes, if CleanLA is delivering the software to public benefit (civic improvement). Hosting is direct program cost.
- **IRS Form 990**: Report under Part I, Program Service Expenses (not overhead).
- **Advantage**: Program expenses improve the nonprofit's program-spending ratio (funders look for > 75% program spend).
- **Detail for attorney**: Clarify in bylaws / conflict-of-interest policy that founder/ED's tech decisions (hosting provider, infrastructure) are arm's length, not self-dealing if founder has tech investments elsewhere.

### Founder as developer post-transfer
**Question**: Can founder remain as unpaid volunteer developer after forming nonprofit? As paid part-time ED?

**Answer**:
- **Volunteer**: Yes; frequent pattern. Founder continues coding, takes no salary.
- **Paid ED**: Yes; more common long-term. Founder hired as ED (with board-approved salary), continues coding as part of job.
- **Paid contractor**: Possible but risky. If founder is contractor (not ED), payments must be arm's length (market rate for contract work), not below-market "sweetheart" deals. Triggers conflict-of-interest scrutiny.

**Best structure**: Founder as paid ED (salary approved by board annually), coding as part of ED responsibilities. Clarify in job description and assignment agreement.

## CA Civic Tech Precedents: IP Transfer Examples

### Code for America (National, CA-based)
- **Status**: 501(c)(3) nonprofit, founded 2009; builds civic tech tools nationwide
- **Notable project**: Clear My Record (helped CA implement automatic record clearance; expanded to 7M+ people cleared across multiple states)
- **IP model**: Code for America holds IP on tools it develops; published code under open-source license (Apache 2.0, typical)
- **Founder transition**: Jennifer Pahlka (founder) transitioned to strategy role after decade of ED tenure; hired external CEO (common governance maturity step)
- **Lesson**: Nationwide civic tech nonprofit *de facto* standard is open-source Apache or GPL; IP transfer to nonprofit happens early

### Code for San Francisco (SF Civic Tech)
- **Status**: 501(c)(3) incorporated April 2024; IRS determination by May 2024 (fast!)
- **Model**: All-volunteer organization; projects built collaboratively (not owned by individual founders)
- **Notable projects**: 
  - Water the Trees (open source, SF tree planting)
  - DVIRC (domestic violence database)
  - Project Safehome (earthquake risk mapper)
- **IP approach**: GitHub repos public under open-source; no single founder owns; nonprofit owns all
- **Founder lesson**: Multiple contributors, no single founder control; board oversight from inception

### Hack for LA (LA Civic Tech Brigade)
- **Status**: Code for America Brigade (national network); affiliated with larger sponsors
- **Model**: Volunteer civic tech; partnerships with LA city gov and nonprofits
- **Projects**: Civic Tech Jobs platform, Civic Tech Structure (meta-project)
- **IP model**: Projects typically owned by Hack for LA or partner organizations; open-source default
- **Founder relevance**: Brigade model distributes leadership; less founder-dependent than single-founder startups

### Lesson across precedents
1. **Open-source is norm**: CA civic tech nonprofits almost always use Apache 2.0 or GPL
2. **Early IP clarity**: Founders transfer code at or near incorporation; no ambiguity later
3. **Board maturity**: Independent boards (not founder-controlled) are expected by funders
4. **Founder transition**: Founder-ED is common Year 1–3, but nonprofits planning growth hire external ED (founder moves to board chair or advisory)

## Common Founder Mistakes in the First Attorney Call

1. **Not clarifying path preference before calling attorney**
   - *Mistake*: Founder hasn't decided 501(c)(3) vs. fiscal sponsorship; wastes attorney billable time exploring both
   - *Fix*: Decide and research before call; ask attorney to confirm fit

2. **Asking attorney to decide whether to form nonprofit**
   - *Mistake*: "Should we form a nonprofit?" is a business/strategy decision, not legal
   - *Fix*: Decide with advisors, stakeholders; ask attorney: "Here's what we want — what's the legal path?"

3. **Wanting to "retain control" without understanding fiduciary duty
   - *Mistake*: "I built this; I should be able to do what I want." Conflicts with board's duty to mission, not founder
   - *Fix*: Acknowledge that 501(c)(3) means accountability to board and public, not just founder's vision

4. **Not clarifying IP ownership upfront
   - *Mistake*: Assuming "my code" transfers automatically; later discovering ambiguity, attorney costs to clean up
   - *Fix*: Bring a list of all code/IP, any licenses, any prior work-for-hire agreements or employer IP claims

5. **Skipping conflict-of-interest policy as "not important"
   - *Mistake*: Form 990 asks about it; absence is red flag for IRS; causes friction when board votes on founder's compensation
   - *Fix*: Draft policy before first board meeting

6. **Confusing fiscal sponsorship with 501(c)(3) incorporation
   - *Mistake*: Thinking fiscal sponsor = nonprofit status for CleanLA; actually sponsor owns 501(c)(3), CleanLA doesn't
   - *Fix*: Attorney should explain difference clearly; ask: "What does 'sponsored project' mean for grants and fundraising?"

7. **Assuming founder can be ED + board chair
   - *Mistake*: Thinks both roles are OK; doesn't anticipate conflicts when board votes on ED salary or direction
   - *Fix*: Ask attorney for governance structure recommendation (e.g., ED non-voting, independent board chair)

8. **Not discussing ongoing cost of 501(c)(3) status
   - *Mistake*: Focuses on incorporation cost, forgets annual Form 990, insurance, compliance
   - *Fix*: Ask attorney to itemize Year 1 and Year 2+ costs upfront

9. **Forgetting to ask about grant eligibility timeline
   - *Mistake*: Assumes nonprofit can grant-raise immediately after incorporation
   - *Fix*: Ask: "When can we realistically start applying for grants?" (Answer: often after 6 months of operations)

10. **Not bringing IP assignment agreement for review before forming nonprofit
    - *Mistake*: Files incorporation, then tries to assign code; creates ambiguous ownership during critical early months
    - *Fix*: Have attorney prepare IP assignment *before* incorporation; sign as part of founding process

1. **Path decision (5 min)**: "We're leaning toward direct 501(c)(3) / fiscal sponsorship. What's your recommendation for a civic-tech software project?"

## Gaps / Open Questions for Attorney

These require licensed-attorney input (cannot be resolved by research alone):
