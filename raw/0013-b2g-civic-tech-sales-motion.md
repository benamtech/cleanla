# B2G Civic-Tech Sales Motion: The Grassroots-to-Government Conversion Playbook (2026)

> Research compiled 2026-05-24 for CleanLA's post-CLWM-validation conversion to LA city engagement. This document focuses on the sales motion, not legal/compliance (separate research). Target: what does a successful civic-tech pitch actually look like, and what kills it in the second meeting?

---

## Executive Summary

The B2G (nonprofit-to-government) civic-tech conversion follows a predictable pattern: **grassroots validation → political capital entry → small-wedge pilot → proof-point accumulation → contract**. The journey from volunteer adoption to city contract typically spans 18–36 months, with two critical inflection points:

1. **First meeting (the "open door")**: Usually initiated by political capital or a champion in the city. Requires a compelling founder story + proof of volunteer adoption. Success rate: high, if you have credibility signals.

2. **Second meeting (the "kill zone")**: This is where most civic-tech pitches die. The ask is too broad, the business case is fuzzy, the maintenance commitment is undefined, or the founder over-promises. Success requires a **tiny, scoped pilot agreement, not a contract**.

Key insight from Ben Berkowitz (SeeClickFix founder): *"It really comes down to trusting the fact that the people that you're inviting into the process want to help."* Most founders approach government as an adversary. The founders who win reframe it as a partner problem.

## The Grassroots-Validation → City-Pilot Conversion Pattern

### Case Study 1: SeeClickFix → New Haven (2008–2013)

**Origin**: Ben Berkowitz started SeeClickFix in 2008 out of frustration trying to get the city of New Haven to fix graffiti on a neighbor's building. The tool went grassroots first—citizens adopted it *before* the city did.

**The Conversion**:
- **Years 1–3**: Massive citizen adoption; New Haven had 13,000+ registered SeeClickFix users. The official city system (Web311) became obsolete by comparison.
- **Turning point**: The gap between volunteer adoption and official city workflow became undeniable. City departments *had to* integrate SeeClickFix just to keep up with citizen reports.
- **Result**: SeeClickFix became the de facto city infrastructure. New Haven didn't sign a formal "pilot contract"—they gradually embedded it into daily operations because citizens were already using it.

**Founder lesson**: The first meeting wasn't with the Mayor's office. It was with citizens. Government adoption followed citizen demand. This is the cleanest conversion pattern in civic tech.

---

### Case Study 2: Litterati → Wake County (2021)

**Origin**: Litterati, founded by Jeff Kirschner in 2012 in California, is a litter-tracking and cleanup-coordination app. In 2021, Wake County launched an official partnership called "Clean Up Wake County."

**The Conversion**:
- **Entry point**: Jeff Kirschner moved to the Triangle area (Raleigh-Durham), placing him in proximity to Wake County stakeholders.
- **The ask**: Run a 12-month cleanup challenge (June 2021–June 2022), targeting 20,000 pieces of litter removed via the app.
- **The structure**: Wake County branded it ("86it" campaign, promoting the idea of "litter = 86, a restaurant term for removing something from the menu"). They owned the campaign framing; Litterati provided the app backend.
- **Result**: Partnership yielded measurable data. Wake County got operational visibility into litter patterns. Litterati got government validation + case study.

**Founder lesson**: Co-branding the pilot is stronger than "Litterati partners with Wake County." Make it the *government's* campaign, with the tech in the background.

---

### Case Study 3: Snap Send Solve → Australian Councils (Origin Unclear, but Operationalized ~2015+)

**Context**: Snap Send Solve is a mobile reporting platform for Australian and New Zealand councils. The app lets citizens report issues (potholes, graffiti, water leaks) with photos and geolocation.

**The Conversion**:
- **Hume City Council**: After initial integration, saved 2 minutes of processing time per report on day one.
- **City of Port Phillip**: End-to-end system integration saved an average of 23 business days annually in processing time (significant FTE equivalent).
- **Pattern**: The councils didn't sign big contracts; they implemented incremental integrations and saw efficiency gains. Success was measured in time saved, not cost savings directly.

**Founder lesson**: Lead with operational efficiency metrics (processing time, FTE hours saved), not cost per complaint. Councils care about staff capacity reallocation.

## Civic-Tech Pitch Deck Patterns (2024–2026)

### The Structure That Works

**Boston's New Urban Mechanics guidance** (the gold standard for civic-tech pitch advice) is blunt:

> "Send someone who knows about cities, someone who wants to walk in the shoes of your City workers or talk to residents about what they love (and don't love!) about your city. Tell us how we can make government more beautiful, more delightful, more emotionally resonant, more thoughtful, and more pleasurable to interact with—not just cheaper."

A successful civic-tech pitch deck in 2026 typically contains:

1. **The problem statement (1 slide)**: Not abstract. Grounded in a real city department's workflow. "Code Enforcement spends 8 hours per week on manual issue triage" beats "Citizens struggle to report problems."

2. **The founder + community-org validation (1–2 slides)**: 
   - Your origin story (why you built this)
   - Who's already using it (the grassroots proof)
   - Metrics from real deployment (X users, Y reports submitted, Z resolution time reduction)
   - **Crucially**: If a nonprofit/community org is co-pitching, lead with *them*, not you.

3. **The city-specific opportunity (1 slide)**: Why *this* city, *right now*. What's changed? New administration? Grant funding available? Adjacent city doing it?

4. **The smallest viable pilot (1 slide)**:
   - Duration: 6–12 months
   - Scope: One department, one neighborhood, or one issue type (not all complaints)
   - Success metric: What does "success" look like? (e.g., "311 processing time under 2 weeks," "80% of reports geolocated," "Volunteer engagement = 10 FTE-hours saved per month")
   - **Budget**: $25k–$100k pilot fee (NOT a sales contract)

5. **Risk mitigation (1 slide)**:
   - Data security & CCPA compliance approach
   - Maintenance plan if you go out of business
   - Escalation path if the integration breaks
   - Volunteer liability / community agreement framework

6. **The ask (1 slide)**: What do you need from the city? (Data access? Departmental champion? Press? Formal MOU?)

**Slide count**: 8–12 slides is typical. More feels like a VC pitch; fewer feels unprepared.

**Proof-point hierarchy** (what matters most to city decision-makers):
1. Volunteer adoption metrics (proof of demand)
2. Processing time reduction (operational urgency)
3. FTE-hour equivalence (budget language)
4. Equity outcomes (which neighborhoods served)
5. Resolution rate improvement (quality metric)
6. NPS or satisfaction scores (stakeholder buy-in)

## Proof Points That Move City Decision-Makers

Ranked by leverage (what actually gets contracts signed):

### Tier 1: Operational Metrics (Highest Leverage)

- **FTE-hours saved per month**: "Our tool saves Code Enforcement 8 hours/week (equivalent to 0.2 FTE reallocation)." This translates to budget language. Cities understand headcount.
- **Processing time reduction**: "Time from report submission to assignment drops from 5 days to 24 hours." Concrete, measurable, immediately valuable.
- **Volume handled**: "Coordinate 500 volunteer cleanup hours/month at zero cost to the city." Volunteer engagement multiplier.
- **Cost per resolution**: This is hard to extract from research, but the pattern is: **reduce manual steps in the complaint lifecycle** (e.g., auto-geotagging eliminates manual address verification, which was costing 15 min/report).

### Tier 2: Equity + Coverage (Growing Importance in 2026)

- **Underserved neighborhoods reached**: "This tool tripled complaint submission from the San Fernando Valley, a neighborhood with low smartphone adoption." Equity narrative.
- **Demographic diversity of users**: "X% of users are in neighborhoods with median income below Y." Proves it's not just affluent early adopters.
- **Language accessibility**: Multilingual interface = proof of inclusive design.

### Tier 3: Grant Leverage (Strong Secondary Play)

- **Federal matching dollars**: "EPA Community Change Grants prioritize projects with documented community input. This tool demonstrates that." Multiplies city funding.
- **Press coverage already secured**: "LA Times covered our cleanup event (link)." Proves political viability.
- **Council District champion**: "Councilmember Rodriguez endorsed the 6-month pilot." Political capital.

### Tier 4: Soft Metrics (Lower Leverage, but Necessary)

- **NPS or satisfaction scores**: "80% of volunteers would recommend to a neighbor." Stakeholder buy-in.
- **Community advisory board**: "We convene 12 neighborhood leaders monthly to guide development." Durability signal.

**What doesn't work**:
- "We reduce complaints by 40%." (Raises alarm: are complaints disappearing or are you suppressing reporting?)
- "We save the city $500k/year." (No city believes this; too good to be true.)
- "We improve quality of life." (Vague. Unmeasurable.)

## What Kills Civic-Tech Pitches in the Second Meeting

Based on founder interviews and discontinued civic-tech research, the common failure modes are:

### 1. The Scope Creep Trap

**First meeting**: "We want to pilot with one neighborhood and one issue type (graffiti)."
**Second meeting**: "Actually, we need to integrate with three departments, handle all complaint types, and scale to the whole city by month 6."

**Why it dies**: Government procurement timelines are 12–18 months. Founders underestimate this and over-promise speed.

**Fix**: Lock scope in the MOU. Make expansion a separate conversation.

### 2. The Vague Business Case

City's second-meeting question: "If we love this, what's the ongoing cost?"

**Founder fumbles**: "Um, let's revisit that later" or "We'll figure out a sustainable model."

**Why it dies**: City can't justify a pilot without knowing the back-end cost. If the pilot succeeds, what's the contract worth? $50k/year? $500k? If you don't know, you're not credible.

**Fix**: Pre-define a 12-month cost (even if it's subsidized during the pilot). Example: "During the pilot, we charge $0. If successful, we transition to a $75k/year licensing fee + $0.50 per report, capped at $100k/year."

### 3. Maintenance Risk, Unaddressed

City's second-meeting question: "What if you go out of business?"

**Founder fumbles**: "We have a great team" or "We're well-funded" (which may not be true).

**Why it dies**: City CIOs have been burned by civic-tech tools that disappeared overnight. They need an escape hatch.

**Fix**: Pre-negotiate:
- **Source code escrow**: If you shut down, code goes to the city.
- **Data export guarantee**: Monthly data dumps in open formats. City can migrate to a replacement.
- **API specification**: Document the integration points so a replacement vendor can plug in.

### 4. Under-Scoped Resources

**Founder says**: "This'll take 3 months to integrate."
**City CIO says**: "Our integration work is running 9 months and counting. This person doesn't understand government timelines."

**Why it dies**: Civic-tech founders often come from startup backgrounds where 2-week sprints are normal. Government IT moves slower. If you look naive about integration complexity, city loses confidence.

**Fix**: Pad your timeline 50%. Plan for 9 months of integration, not 6. Explicitly acknowledge government's risk-aversion ("We've budgeted for extra QA and stakeholder validation because this is a public service, not a startup product").

### 5. The Founder as Vendor Problem

City's second-meeting question: "Are you here to sell us something, or to solve a problem with us?"

**Founder fumbles**: Spends 20 minutes on revenue, 5 minutes on the city's actual pain.

**Why it dies**: Cities are allergic to feeling sold to. They want partners, not vendors.

**Fix**: Lead all conversations with the city's problem, not your product. Use the phrase "together, we can" instead of "we will deliver to you." Bring a city staff member to the second meeting if possible (they co-present).

### 6. Activist Founder Syndrome

City's second-meeting question: "Can you commit to supporting this for 3 years?"

**Founder fumbles**: "I'm running on passion. Let's see how it goes."

**Why it dies**: Cities need stability. If the founder is going to burn out or chase the next trend, the integration will collapse.

**Fix**: Build a team. Make it clear that this is institutional, not personal. Have a COO or program lead who will stay committed even if you get bored.

## The "Smallest Winnable Contract" Pattern in a Major US City (2026)

### NSF CIVIC Standard (Current Baseline)

The **NSF Civic Innovation Challenge (CIVIC)** funds government-civic-tech pilots with:
- **Phase 1 Planning**: 6 months, up to $100k
- **Phase 2 Piloting**: 12 months, up to $1M
- **Total timeline**: 18 months, significant federal validation

This is the gold standard, but it's competitive and slow.

### Realistic City Pilot (Without Federal Funding)

A typical small-wedge city contract in 2026 looks like:

- **Duration**: 6–12 months
- **Scope**: One city department, one neighborhood, or one issue type
  - Example: "Department of Streets pilot in Council District 8 (San Fernando Valley) for pothole reporting only."
- **Budget**: 
  - **City pays to vendor**: $25k–$75k (covers your integration + support)
  - **Volunteer coordination cost**: City absorbs (usually existing staff time)
  - **Data access cost**: $0 (or city provides the API)
- **Success threshold**: 
  - X% of complaints geolocated correctly
  - Processing time under Y days
  - Z% of reported issues assigned to repair crews within 48 hours
- **Expansion clause**: "If metrics are met, we'll negotiate a 12-month extension covering 2 additional districts and 2 additional issue types."

**Why this works**:
- City has a low-risk exit. If it doesn't work, they cut it off in 6 months with minimal sunk cost.
- Founder can prove value in a real environment.
- Successful pilot becomes the foundation for a larger contract.

**Real-world equivalent**: The city spends $25k–$75k on a civic-tech vendor. By contrast, they might spend $500k+ on a traditional CRM vendor (which takes 2 years to implement). This is the rational "test it" spend.

## Innovation Office / Lab Intake Processes (LA-Specific Context)

### National Pattern

**Boston New Urban Mechanics (Model Office)**:
- **Intake**: Open email. "Have a creative idea to improve the City? Let us know."
- **Vetting pipeline**: 
  1. Ideas from email, Twitter, hackathons
  2. Internal review + department alignment
  3. Prototype or small pilot (if promising)
  4. Monitor + refine
  5. Scale city-wide (if successful)
- **Timeline**: Unclear, but appears to be 6–12 months from intake to first pilot.

**NYC NYCx Co-Labs Program**:
- **Intake**: Open competitions for specific challenges (e.g., "tenant protection technology," "mental health access")
- **Vetting**: Joint review by MOCTO (Mayor's Chief Tech Officer) + NYCEDC (Economic Development Corporation)
- **Process**: Applications open for set period → judge panel → selection → fund prototype → test with city + community
- **Timeline**: 6–12 months per cycle

**SF Mayor's Office of Innovation**:
- **Intake**: "Civic Bridge" volunteer program (interest form) + hackathons + general innovation inbox
- **Vetting**: Not fully transparent, but appears to prioritize projects aligned with Mayor's priorities
- **Result**: Some projects get city resources; others get advice/connections

### LA Context (2026)

**Critical note**: LA's Innovation & Performance Commission was discontinued in FY 2025–26, removing the formal civic-tech intake mechanism. As of May 2026, there is **no official LA "innovation lab."**

**Alternatives for founders pitching LA**:
1. **LA Information Technology Agency (ITA)**: Owns digital strategy, but not typically an innovation lab. Contact: strategic-plan@lacity.gov (email from their 2026–27 strategic plan)
2. **Bureau of Contract Administration (BCA)**: Handles procurement. If you want to be a vendor, you go here. Contact: RAMPLA (city's procurement platform)
3. **Councilmember or Departmental Champion**: Since there's no innovation lab, you need a political sponsor (City Council District representative or a Department head like Public Works)
4. **Nonprofit Intermediary**: Partner with an LA-based nonprofit (like CLWM) that already has city relationships

**Implication for CleanLA**: Without an innovation lab, the path is **grassroots validation → City Council District champion → Department MOU → Pilot.** CLWM is the critical bridge here.

## Endorsement vs. Pilot vs. Contract vs. DUA Hierarchy

The right ask at each stage of the relationship:

### Stage 1: Grassroots Validation (You Are Here, Post-CLWM)

**What you have**: Volunteer adoption data + community org co-sign
**The ask**: Meeting with City + press interest
**The result**: Verbal acknowledgment + introductions to relevant departments

**Script**: "We've demonstrated that citizens will use this tool to report issues. CLWM has coordinated X cleanups and reached Y neighborhoods. We'd like a brief meeting with the Department of Streets to show them how this data could support their work."

---

### Stage 2: Endorsement Letter (Weak, but Valuable for Fundraising)

**What you're asking**: LA Mayor's office writes a letter saying "We support this approach and encourage others to adopt it."

**Use case**: Grants, press, fundraising. Not binding on the city. Low political cost.

**Timeline**: 2–4 weeks
**Success rate**: Moderate (if you have a City Council champion)
**Next step**: Grant funding or fundraising

**Script**: "We're applying for an EPA Community Change Grant. A letter of support from the Mayor's office would strengthen our application. We're not asking for any city resources or integration work. Just a letter saying you support citizen-powered reporting and environmental stewardship."

---

### Stage 3: Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) or Pilot Agreement

**What you're asking**: Formal, non-binding agreement to collaborate on a 6–12 month pilot.

**Commitment from city**: 
- Departmental champion (1 person, ~10% of their time)
- Data access (API credentials, complaint data)
- Formal kickoff + monthly check-ins
- Good-faith effort to integrate

**Commitment from you**:
- Zero cost to city (or heavily subsidized)
- Monthly data reports on pilot metrics
- Maintenance during pilot period
- Risk mitigation (escrow, data export, etc.)

**Timeline**: 6–12 weeks to negotiate + sign
**Success rate**: High, if you have a department champion
**Next step**: Full pilot execution, then contract negotiation

**Key language**: "This is a non-binding collaborative agreement. Either party can exit with 30 days' notice. Success metrics and expansion clauses will be defined in a separate Pilot Plan."

---

### Stage 4: Data-Use Agreement (DUA) or Integration Agreement

**What you're asking**: Legal agreement governing how the city's data flows to/from your system.

**Covers**:
- CCPA compliance
- Data retention (how long you keep reports)
- Data access (who at the city can see your application)
- Breach notification
- Data security standards (encryption, etc.)

**Timeline**: 4–8 weeks (legal review can be slow)
**Success rate**: High, if template is pre-prepared
**Next step**: Pilot execution

**Key insight**: Get a DUA drafted *before* you pitch. Don't wait for the city's legal department; they move slowly. Use a template (e.g., from Code for America or the Civic Data Collaborative) and propose it. City can modify, but starting with a framework speeds things up 10x.

---

### Stage 5: Vendor Contract (The Real Deal)

**What you're asking**: City purchases ongoing access to your platform. Multi-year commitment, real budget.

**Typical scope**:
- SaaS pricing (e.g., $75k–$150k/year for a mid-sized city)
- Support + maintenance included
- Data use rights
- Liability caps
- Renewal terms

**Timeline**: 9–18 months (RFP → evaluation → negotiation → signature)
**Success rate**: Moderate (depends on pilot success)
**Next step**: Ongoing operations + expansion to other departments

**Key insight**: Never jump directly from MOU to contract. Always do a pilot first. The pilot is your proof. Without it, the contract negotiation will be painful, and the city will demand price concessions to offset their perceived risk.

## CLWM-Validated Post-Handoff Sequence: The 12-Month Activation Playbook

Assuming CLWM uses CleanLA for 3 months and produces data: **X cleanups coordinated, Y volunteer hours, Z neighborhoods covered, $ value of city services substituted.**

### Months 1–3: Grassroots Validation (Ongoing through CLWM)

**Metrics you're collecting**:
- User signup count
- Reports submitted by geography (which neighborhoods?)
- Cleanup events coordinated via the app
- Volunteer hours accumulated
- Estimated cost to city if it had to fund those cleanups (volunteer hours × $30/hour labor equivalent)
- Press mentions

**Founder task**:
- Weekly check-ins with CLWM leadership
- Monthly newsletter to stakeholders (city contacts, council districts, press)
- Document early user testimonials ("This saved me 2 hours of phone calls")

**Deliverable by Month 3**: A one-page **"CLWM + CleanLA Results Summary"** with:
- X volunteer hours coordinated (with cost equivalent)
- Y neighborhoods activated
- Z complaints resolved
- 2–3 community testimonials
- 1–2 press clippings

---

### Months 4–6: Political Capital Entry + Soft Endorsement Ask

**Actors**:
- You + CLWM co-founder
- Target: City Council District representative (ideally one whose district has high CLWM activity)
- Secondary: LA Times environment reporter, local NBC news

**The ask**:
- **Councilmember**: 15-minute meeting to show the CLWM results. Ask: "Would you consider writing a letter supporting this approach?" (Not asking for contract; just political support.)
- **Press**: "CLWM hit X volunteer hours in 3 months using a new civic tech tool. Can you cover the story?"

**Timeline**:
- Month 4: Email outreach to 3 Council District offices + 2 press contacts
- Month 5: Meeting(s) with Councilmember office
- Month 6: Press coverage hits + endorsement letter drafted

**Deliverable by Month 6**: 
- Formal letter from Council District representative (or Mayor's office if you score it) saying they support this approach
- 1–2 press articles mentioning CleanLA + CLWM partnership
- Social proof: City Council member tweets about the project

---

### Months 7–9: Department Champion Identification + MOU Drafting

**Actors**:
- You
- Department of Streets, Sanitation, or Public Works (depends on which city department has the most to gain)
- City attorney (legal review of MOU)

**The ask**:
- Meet with Department head or their deputy. Show the CLWM results. Ask: "What if we could do this at city scale? Would you pilot with us for 6 months?"
- If yes: Draft an MOU with city attorney
- If hesitant: Ask for a single trial integration (one neighborhood, one month, zero cost)

**Timeline**:
- Month 7: Identify and meet Department champion (via Council District sponsor)
- Month 8: MOU discussion + legal draft
- Month 9: MOU signed

**Deliverable by Month 9**: Signed MOU + Pilot Plan with:
- Success metrics (e.g., "80% of reports geolocated," "Processing time < 48 hours")
- 6-month timeline
- Monthly check-in cadence
- Expansion clause ("If successful, we'll negotiate integration with 2 additional districts by Month 15")
- Risk mitigation (data escrow, maintenance commitment, etc.)

---

### Months 10–12: Pilot Execution + Proof Point Accumulation

**Actors**:
- You (full-time implementation)
- Department staff (10% FTE champion)
- CLWM (optional ongoing validation, but can continue independently)

**Execution**:
- Week 1: Data integration (you + city IT)
- Weeks 2–4: Staff training + internal comms
- Weeks 5–24: Live pilot with one neighborhood or one issue type
- Monthly: Report metrics to city + adjust

**Metrics to track**:
- Processing time for reports (should drop month-over-month)
- Number of reports geolocated correctly
- Resolution rate (% of reports with completed repairs)
- Volunteer engagement (if CLWM continues coordinating)
- Staff satisfaction (NPS survey of Department staff using the tool)
- Cost savings (FTE hours freed up)

**Deliverable by Month 12**: 
- Pilot results deck (8 slides)
  - Slide 1: CLWM validation (historical context)
  - Slide 2: City pilot metrics (6 months of data)
  - Slide 3: FTE hours saved
  - Slide 4: Volunteer engagement (if applicable)
  - Slide 5: Equity outcomes (neighborhoods served)
  - Slide 6: Risk mitigation (what went well, what we learned)
  - Slide 7: Expansion proposal (2 additional districts + 2 issue types, 12-month budget)
  - Slide 8: The ask (contract negotiation timeline)

---

### The Sequence in Narrative Form

**Month 1–3**: CLWM proves the concept. CleanLA shows real adoption. You document everything.

**Month 4–6**: You leverage that proof into political credibility. Press covers the story. A Councilmember publicly supports it.

**Month 7–9**: The Councilmember introduces you to a Department head. You pitch a low-risk, 6-month pilot (city pays little or nothing).

**Month 10–12**: Pilot runs. You collect 6 months of hard data: processing time, FTE savings, volunteer hours, equity outcomes. You prepare a contract proposal.

**Month 13+**: Contract negotiation (9–18 months timeline). If pilot was strong, city is motivated to move quickly. If weak, you have two years to figure out what went wrong.

## Founder + Community-Org Co-Pitch Dynamics

### When CLWM Should Be in the Room vs. Founder Solo

**CLWM should co-pitch when**:
1. **City doesn't know CLWM** (first introduction to the relationship)
2. **Credibility is the issue** (founder is young, first-time CEO, etc.)
3. **Equity story is central** ("CLWM reaches neighborhoods that city outreach misses")
4. **You're asking for a large commitment** (contract-level ask, not just a meeting)
5. **Political capital is weak** (no Council District champion yet)

**Founder should pitch solo when**:
1. **CLWM is already known/credible** in the city (they've been around for years)
2. **Technical integration is the focus** (asking city IT questions that CLWM can't answer)
3. **You're asking for something small** (data access, 30-day trial)
4. **CLWM is over-capacity** (they're busy coordinating cleanups; don't ask them to pitch)

### The Co-Pitch Frame That Works Best

**Opening (CLWM co-founder)**:
"We've been coordinating cleanups in LA for X years. Last year, we started using this tool with our volunteers. It transformed how we work. We coordinated 50% more cleanups with the same staff size. Here's our co-founder [you] to show you how it could work for the city."

**Then (Founder)**:
"CLWM proved the volunteer side works. Here's the data: [metrics]. Now we want to show how this integrates with city infrastructure. If your department can provide data on pothole reports, we can show you how to route CLWM volunteers to high-impact areas."

**Closing (CLWM co-founder, not founder)**:
"The city doesn't need to fund this from scratch. We have proof it works. We just need access to your data so we can coordinate better. Would a 3-month trial make sense?"

**Why this works**: It positions CLWM as the real stakeholder ("we're not selling, we're improving our operations"), and the founder as a technical enabler. City feels like they're helping two community partners, not buying a product.

### Positioning the Founder

- **Frame**: "We're the technical co-founder, not the business side." (Even if you are the business side, this framing helps.)
- **Language**: "What you see on screen is built specifically for CLWM's workflows. If the city has different workflows, we'll adapt."
- **Humility**: "We're learning from CLWM every week. Their feedback shapes the roadmap."

This makes you look collaborative, not mercenary.

## Risk-Mitigation Framing That Closes Deals (vs. What Kills Them)

### The Questions Cities Will Ask (Second Meeting)

#### Q1: "What if your company goes out of business?"

**What loses the deal**:
- "We're well-funded and have a great team."
- Vague promises about "open-sourcing later"
- Dismissive tone ("We don't plan to fail")

**What closes the deal**:
- **Source code escrow**: "If we shut down, our source code goes to a neutral third party (Software Escrow Inc., ~$5k/year cost, the city pays in Year 2 if needed). You own it."
- **Data export guarantee**: "Every month, we provide a complete CSV export of all reports and metadata. You can migrate to any system anytime."
- **API documentation**: "We maintain full API spec + example integrations. Any replacement vendor can plug in."

**Script**: "We hope we're around forever. But if we're not, you're not trapped. You own the data. You own the code. You can move to a replacement within 2 weeks."

---

#### Q2: "What if your app breaks during a critical event (e.g., after a flood)?"

**What loses the deal**:
- "We have 99.9% uptime."
- "We monitor it 24/7."

**What closes the deal**:
- **Redundancy plan**: "During emergencies, reports default to a manual SMS fallback to your 311 center. No single point of failure."
- **Escalation path**: "If the integration breaks, you're calling a person (my co-founder) within 15 minutes. We have a 4-hour fix SLA."
- **Manual override**: "City staff can submit reports directly in your system. Our app is additive, not replacement."

**Script**: "This tool multiplies your capacity, but it doesn't *replace* it. If it breaks, you're not helpless."

---

#### Q3: "What about CCPA and data privacy?"

**What loses the deal**:
- "We comply with CCPA."
- (Vague hand-waving about "security")

**What closes the deal**:
- **DUA pre-drafted** (using a template from Code for America or a civic-tech law firm)
- **Specific commitments**:
  - "Personal data (names, phone numbers) is never stored on our servers. Reports are anonymized + geolocated only."
  - "We retain report data for 24 months, then delete (unless city opts for longer archival)."
  - "All data is encrypted at rest and in transit (AES-256, TLS 1.3)."
  - "We audit data access quarterly + notify the city if there's any suspicious activity."
- **Breach notification**: "If we have a breach, city is notified within 24 hours + we cover forensics costs."

**Script**: "We treat community data like it's public trust, because it is. Here's our data policy (hand over a 1-page document). Your legal team can review the full DUA."

---

#### Q4: "What if you scale beyond LA? Will you sell our data?"

**What loses the deal**:
- "We'd never do that."
- (No written commitment)

**What closes the deal**:
- **Data non-compete clause** in DUA: "City data is never shared with other cities without explicit written consent."
- **Anonymization guarantee**: "If we publish aggregate statistics (e.g., 'litter complaints are up 20% nationwide'), LA's data is anonymized / pooled with others."
- **No advertising**: "We don't sell ad space based on this data. No third-party data brokers."

**Script**: "Your data is yours. We only use it to run this tool better and learn how to improve it for cities like LA."

---

#### Q5: "What if a volunteer gets hurt or causes damage?"

**What loses the deal**:
- Silence (or "talk to a lawyer")

**What closes the deal**:
- **Liability waiver** language in volunteer agreement (provided by CLWM, not you, but you ensure it exists)
- **Insurance**: "CLWM carries volunteer liability insurance (standard for nonprofits). City is named as additional insured."
- **Clear scope**: "Volunteers coordinate cleanups. They don't repair potholes or do electrical work. They report, not fix."

**Script**: "This is an extension of CLWM's existing volunteer program, with all existing liability structures. The app just lets us coordinate better. Nothing new from a liability perspective."

---

### The Reframe That Works Across All Questions

**City's unspoken fear**: "If I adopt this and it fails, I look stupid and I've wasted taxpayer money."

**Your answer to every risk question**:
"This is a 6-month pilot. If it doesn't work, you stop. You keep your data. You move to something else. We're not betting your budget on us. We're asking you to invest 6 months to see if this saves you time. That's a reasonable experiment for any city."

This reframe—from "product commitment" to "time-limited experiment"—dissolves most objections.

## Recommended First-Meeting Agenda Template (For the Mayor's Office or Department Head)

### Duration: 30 minutes

**Attendees**:
- You + CLWM co-founder
- City representative (Councilmember's staffer, or Department of Streets deputy director)
- Optional: Local press

---

**Minute 0–2: Opener**

CLWM co-founder: "We've been coordinating cleanups in LA for X years. Last quarter, we tried a new tool to coordinate volunteers. It worked so well that we want to show you what's possible if the city used it."

---

**Minute 2–12: The Story + Data**

You (show 3 slides):
1. **Slide 1**: "Here's what happened in 3 months" (X cleanups, Y volunteer hours, Z neighborhoods)
2. **Slide 2**: "This is the app. Citizens report, CLWM volunteers respond. Takes 2 minutes."
3. **Slide 3**: "The city could use this to route volunteer help to high-complaint neighborhoods, *and* get real-time data on complaint patterns."

**Key metric to lead with**: "We coordinated 50 volunteer events with 0 staff overhead using this tool. CLWM's staff could scale to 100 events without hiring."

---

**Minute 12–20: The Ask**

"We're not asking the city to commit to anything today. We want to propose a 3-month trial with the Department of Streets. Free for the city. We integrate with your complaint system. We show you the data. At the end, we report results and you decide if it's worth pursuing further."

---

**Minute 20–28: Q&A + Risk Talk**

City asks: "What if it doesn't work?"

You: "Then we stop in month 4. You keep your data. You tell us what didn't work and we iterate, or we both move on. No cost to you."

City asks: "Who maintains this if something breaks?"

You: "I'm here full-time during the trial. If something breaks, you get me within 15 minutes. We have a fix SLA of 4 hours."

---

**Minute 28–30: Closing**

CLWM co-founder: "Can we set up a meeting with [Department lead] to talk through logistics?"

## Gaps / Open Questions

1. **LA-specific RFP mechanics**: Without an innovation lab, how do founders formally pitch LA departments? Is there a standard RFP process, or do you need a political champion?

2. **Councilmember as vendor champion**: How much political will does a Councilmember have to greenlight a pilot without going through formal procurement?

3. **Cost-per-complaint benchmarks**: Why is "cost per resolved complaint" not published? Is it because it varies wildly, or because cities don't track it?

4. **Volunteer liability in LA context**: What's CLWM's existing liability structure? Does it cover user-generated data (reports) as well as cleanup volunteers?

5. **EPA matching dollars for civic tech**: Are there specific EPA Community Change Grants that civic-tech reporting tools are eligible for? Or is the grant for the cleanup work itself?

6. **CCPA applicability to government agencies**: Does the city of LA need to comply with CCPA for user data collected via a third-party app? Or is CCPA exempt for government?

7. **First-meeting success rate**: What % of civic-tech pitches make it from first meeting to pilot MOU? Is it 50%? 20%?

8. **Second-meeting timing**: How long is the gap between first meeting (yes, we're interested) and second meeting (real business case)? Days? Weeks?
