# Federal + State Funding Alignment for LA Civic Tech (2026)

> Compiled 2026-05-24 for CleanLA's B2G activation playbook. Goal: make the Mayor's-office pitch carry funding upside, not just cost. The strategic insight: a citizen-reported street-issue data tool becomes infinitely more valuable to the city when it unlocks matching dollars, federal grant leverage, and alignment with existing city federal-money pipelines. This research maps the federal and state programs CleanLA could feed into or amplify for Los Angeles.

## Executive summary

Los Angeles has extraordinary federal and state funding flowing into the city across homelessness, infrastructure, environmental justice, and disaster recovery. Most of it requires data, reporting, community engagement, and geospatial targeting—exactly what CleanLA provides. The pitch is not "use our tool" but "use our tool AND unlock $X in federal/state matching dollars, fast-track your grant compliance reporting, and align with the 2028 Olympics infrastructure sprint." Key finding: $3B+ in direct federal/state programs are already allocated to LA; the barrier is not availability but leverage, matching capacity, and the data systems that unlock them.

## Federal programs CleanLA could plug into

### Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) — $millions annually

**Status:** Live, ongoing. LA County administers both City of LA and County-wide allocations.

**How it works:** HUD distributes ~$4B nationally in CDBG annually. LA City and LA County receive allocations directly. Funds must benefit low/moderate-income persons (≥70% of spend). Eligible uses include graffiti removal, street repairs, homelessness, business loans, community services.

**CleanLA fit:** Citizen-reported street issues (graffiti, blight, illegal dumping, pothole) feed directly into CDBG project prioritization. CDBG reporting requirements demand baseline data, target-area mapping, and outcome tracking. CleanLA provides this free or at near-zero marginal cost to the city. Pitch: "Use CleanLA to democratize street-issue reporting, target CDBG cleanup spend to highest-need areas, and meet HUD's mandatory data-driven allocation reporting."

**LA-specific impact:** Not disclosed in search results, but CDBG allocations typically represent $50–200M/year per major city. Worth requesting directly from LA County Community Development Authority.

**Source:** [HUD CDBG](https://www.hud.gov/hud-partners/community-cdbg), [LACDA](https://www.lacda.org/community-development/cdbg)

### CDBG Disaster Recovery (CDBG-DR) — $2B+ flowing for 2025 LA fires

**Status:** Activated. Federal government allocated $110B in disaster-recovery funding; LA fires triggered rapid CDBG-DR authorization.

**How it works:** HUD awards block grants to states and cities for long-term recovery from major disasters. Unlike FEMA (immediate relief), CDBG-DR funds infrastructure repair, community recovery, blight reduction, and housing.

**CleanLA fit:** Post-encampment-clearance sanitation, street cleanup and debris removal tracking, illegal dumping enforcement in fire-recovery zones. Citizens report cleanup needs post-fire; city uses CleanLA to triage, track, and report outcomes to HUD. Reduces manual data entry, enables faster grant drawdown.

**2025 LA fires status:** As of March 2026, federal aid exceeded $2B (FEMA + SBA). CDBG-DR authorization expected but timing uncertain (Congress gridlock has historically delayed CDBG-DR); worth tracking HUD announcements.

**Critical note:** CDBG-DR is extremely slow to deploy (18+ months typical). But it is a large, dedicated bucket. Early-stage civic-tech integration could accelerate grant execution.

**Source:** [HUD CDBG-DR](https://www.hud.gov/hud-partners/community-cdbg-dr), [Carnegie Endowment on CA disaster recovery](https://carnegieendowment.org/emissary/2025/01/la-california-wildfires-disaster-recovery?lang=en), [Governor Newsom fire response](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/01/13/governor-newsom-proposes-billions-in-new-funding-for-l-a-firestorm-response-and-recovery/)

### Emergency Solutions Grants (ESG) — LA County allocation, homeless outreach

**Status:** Live, ongoing.

**How it works:** HUD awards ESG to cities and states for homeless street outreach, emergency shelter, rapid rehousing, and prevention. Eligible activities include street outreach costs, connecting unsheltered people to services, HMIS data systems, and administrative overhead (≤7.5%).

**CleanLA fit:** Many street issues (illegal dumping, biohazard sites, encampment sanitation) are reported by or require coordination with homeless outreach teams. CleanLA acts as a "router" for outreach personnel—citizen-reported unsafe/unhygenic street conditions feed into ESG-funded LAHSA street outreach, which triages response. Data integration improves ESG program reporting (HUD requires outcome tracking).

**LA-specific allocation:** LA Homeless Services Authority (LAHSA) administers ESG for LA City and County. Specific FY 2025-26 allocation amount not disclosed in search results; worth requesting from LAHSA directly.

**Source:** [HUD ESG Program](https://www.hudexchange.info/programs/esg/), [LAHSA](https://www.lhc.la.gov), [LACDA ESG](https://www.lacda.org/homelessness/emergency-solutions-grant)

### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL) — $160M to LA region for streets/sidewalks/equity

**Status:** Live, rolling deployment through 2026.

**How it works:** $110B for surface transportation (roads, transit, sidewalks, bike lanes, safety). Emphasis on equity: $100M/year (5-year authorization) specifically for urban tree cover in low-income neighborhoods. Local governments apply for discretionary grants or receive formula allocations through state DOT.

**CleanLA fit:** BIL-funded street/sidewalk projects require baseline condition mapping, community input, and post-completion verification. CleanLA provides crowd-sourced street-condition data at zero marginal cost, improving project targeting and community buy-in.

**LA-specific allocations:**
- $160M in new federal grants for street/transit infrastructure, traffic safety, and neighborhood connectivity in LA region (source: Mayor Bass announcement, 2026)
- Urban tree canopy grants: $100M/year federal authorization for trees in poorest neighborhoods nationwide; LA's share TBD but significant given LA's equity focus

**2028 Olympics angle:** BIL funds are accelerating completion of "Twenty-Eight by '28" infrastructure projects (Purple Line extension, Gold Line expansion, Complete Streets). CleanLA's data could feed into final-mile beautification and public-space readiness before Games.

**Source:** [BIL California overview](https://levin.house.gov/imo/media/doc/Bipartisan_Infrastructure_Law_California.pdf), [Mayor Bass $900M announcement](https://mayor.lacity.gov/news/major-support-secured-los-angeles-region-receive-nearly-900-million-funding-strengthen), [US Forest Service BIL trees](https://www.fs.usda.gov/fs-tags/bipartisan-infrastructure-law)

### Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) — $3B environmental justice grants, $1.6B awarded to 105 projects nationwide

**Status:** Active. EPA Phase 1 (~$1.6B for 105 projects) announced Nov 2024. Phase 2 rolling out through 2026.

**How it works:** Section 60201 appropriates $2.8B for environmental and climate justice grants to nonprofits, tribes, local governments, and their partnerships. Eligible uses: pollution monitoring, remediation, climate resilience, workforce development, community engagement.

**CleanLA fit:** Illegal dumping, litter accumulation, and blight are environmental justice issues. Citizen-reported data feeds EPA environmental justice grant narratives. LA nonprofits or city could co-apply for IRA grants with CleanLA as the data backbone, reporting outcomes of community-led cleanup initiatives.

**LA-specific allocation:** Search results do not list LA-specific awardees from Phase 1. EPA maintains a grants database; worth checking for LA environmental justice organizations that received 2024-2025 awards (potential partnerships).

**Critical timing:** Phase 2 RFPs expected 2026-2027; CleanLA could position as the "community monitoring platform" for environmental justice initiatives.

**Source:** [EPA IRA Environmental Justice](https://www.epa.gov/inflation-reduction-act/inflation-reduction-act-environmental-and-climate-justice-program), [IRA Tracker](https://iratracker.org/programs/ira-section-60201-environmental-and-climate-justice-block-grants/)

## California state programs

### CalRecycle Grants — illegal dumping cleanup + local conservation corps

**Status:** Active. FY 2025-26 funding cycles open or recently opened.

**Allocations:**
- **Local Conservation Corps (LCC) Grant:** $29.7M for FY 2025-26. Supports 14 LCC programs statewide to train youth 18-25 in recycling, waste tire collection, and illegal dumping cleanup. Frequent projects: waste tire collection and hauling on public/private land.
- **Illegal Disposal Site Abatement Grant:** Up to $500K per applicant per fiscal year. Funds cleanup of sites where responsible party is absent or unable to remediate. Application deadline for Cycle 104 (FY 2025-26): Oct 15, 2025 (past; next cycle likely Fall 2026).

**CleanLA fit:** LA city submits CDBG-funded illegal dumping cleanup projects. CalRecycle grant applications require documentation of illegal sites. CleanLA provides crowd-sourced mapping and reporting of dumping hotspots, reducing the city's cost to identify and prioritize sites for remediation. Also: data from CleanLA helps coordinate LCC crew deployments.

**Critical note:** These grants are available to public entities (city is eligible direct applicant). The barrier is often staff capacity to write grant applications and track outcomes—CleanLA's data dashboard reduces this burden.

**Source:** [CalRecycle LCC](https://calrecycle.ca.gov/funding/localcc/fy202526/), [CalRecycle Illegal Dumping](https://calrecycle.ca.gov/lea/grantsloans/solidwaste/lea/fy202425/)

## Federal data/dashboard grant programs

### OMB Uniform Guidance: federal funds CAN pay for data systems

**Key insight:** OMB Uniform Guidance (2 CFR 200) explicitly permits federal grant recipients to fund "data costs," including:
- Data systems and platforms
- Personnel for data management
- Dashboards and reporting tools
- Cybersecurity and data integration

**Implication:** Any federal grant (CDBG, BIL, ESG, IRA, etc.) that a city receives can legally allocate a portion to fund a civic-data platform like CleanLA IF it supports grant compliance or program delivery.

**CleanLA pitch:** Position as "CDBG/BIL/ESG compliance and outcome-reporting backbone." City can fund CleanLA maintenance/development directly from grant overhead (up to 10-15% of award in many programs).

**National examples:**
- Seattle Adopt-A-Street (resident-driven street cleanup, volunteer coordination)
- Data-Smart City Solutions (Harvard Kennedy School resource on civic data use cases)
- National League of Cities Federal Grant Equity Dashboard (connects cities to grant opportunities)

**Source:** [OMB Uniform Guidance](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-2/subtitle-A/chapter-II/part-200), [Data-Smart City Solutions](https://datasmart.hks.harvard.edu/how-can-data-and-analytics-be-used-to-enhance-city-operations-723), [NLC article](https://www.nlc.org/article/2024/06/21/how-one-data-dashboard-helps-small-cities-leverage-federal-funding/)

## The "matching dollar" reality

### Why matching dollars matter

Most federal grants (CDBG, BIL, IRA, Measure A, CalRecycle) require or incentivize local matching. Common ratios:

- **50% federal, 50% local match:** City must fund half the project cost from own funds.
- **80/20 or 90/10:** More favorable, but usually for small grants or disadvantaged communities.

**The barrier:** LA City often has money for matching but must go through lengthy appropriation processes (City Council, CAO approval). State and federal funds sit idle while city "finds" match. Multi-month delays compound.

**CleanLA angle:** If CleanLA is framed as the *data infrastructure* that accelerates grant execution (faster targeting, compliance reporting, outcome tracking), the city saves months of administrative overhead. Faster grant drawdown = faster benefits to residents and faster match-clearing.

### LA City 2025-26 budget constraints

- Inside Safe already consumed $322M+ with uneven results
- Mayor Bass's emergency declaration ending; budget turning to structural deficits
- Measure A (County homelessness tax) provides some relief but doesn't fund city operations
- City matching capacity is tight; grants requiring large city matches are deprioritized

**Implication for CleanLA:** Pitch as "the tool that lets the city do more with federal money" — faster execution, better outcomes per dollar, proven outcome reporting. This reduces political friction around accepting new grant obligations.
