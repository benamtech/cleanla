# Civic-Tech Founder → Community-Org Handoff Case Studies (2026)

> Compiled 2026-05-24 for CleanLA's Phase 1.5 partnership conversation with Clean LA With Me. Goal: pattern-match successful and failed handoffs to inform the founder's first 30-60 minute conversation with Juan Naula.

## Executive summary

The strongest signal across all successful cases is **listening first, building second**. SeeClickFix grew through organic adoption in New Haven because Ben Berkowitz solved his own problem *before* asking government to adopt it. FixMyStreet and mySociety succeeded because they designed with—not for—local councils. The fatal pattern in failures (Joshua Tauberer's projects, Snapcrap, Code for America Brigade sunset) is **founder-centric ideation**: the technologist builds what they think the problem needs, then looks for users. The second-order pattern: **IP and credit clarity matters less than decision-making power clarity**. Community-led projects (mRelief, Litterati) thrive when the nonprofit org controls roadmap, not just receives a finished product. The first conversation with Naula should confirm: does Juan want *ownership and control*, or a *tool*? That distinction determines the entire partnership structure.

## Direct precedent: successful handoffs

### SeeClickFix (Ben Berkowitz, New Haven, 2007–2019)

Berkowitz built SeeClickFix because graffiti on State Street stayed unfixed. He solved his own problem first. The app reduced reporting time to two clicks (vs. 60+ seconds on SF311). Word spread organically through residents; City Hall saw the adoption data, then *requested* to be integrated. The pitch was never "I built a tool, will you use it?" — it was "Residents love this; how do we make it official?"

Key product decisions reflected government workflow, not just citizen enthusiasm: a "thank you" button so residents could praise officials, refusal to delete archived data when a city attorney requested it, dynamic mobile apps that allowed iteration without app-store resubmission.

Acquired by CivicPlus in 2019; Berkowitz stayed as director. The partnership with government had been embedded for 12 years before the commercial exit.

**Sources:** [6 Years On, SeeClickFix Has Changed New Haven](https://www.newhavenindependent.org/2013/11/22/seeclickfix_6_years/), [SeeClickFix Sold, Staying In New Haven](https://www.newhavenindependent.org/2019/10/14/seeclickfix/), [The 11 Product Decisions That Helped SeeClickFix](https://medium.com/@benberkowitz/the-10-product-decisions-that-helped-seeclickfix-and-its-government-partners-succeed-f243d0f23fb7)

### FixMyStreet / mySociety (Tom Steinberg, UK, 2007–present)

Steinberg founded mySociety as a UK charity in 2003. FixMyStreet launched 2007-02-02. The tool made councils' jobs easier: citizens did the frontline reporting; councils received structured, geotagged, validated reports instead of scattered phone calls. Councils integrated; word spread. In 2012, FixMyStreet for Councils launched — white-label, embeddable, open-source. Steinberg stepped down 2015 with the org "in good shape." The "handoff" was succession, not sale.

**Why it worked:** Built as a charity from the start (no profit-extraction pressure), open-source code (low switching costs), founder did not try to be indispensable.

**Sources:** [FixMyStreet.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FixMyStreet.com), [mySociety – Participedia](https://participedia.net/organization/mysociety), [History / mySociety](https://www.mysociety.org/about/history/)

### mRelief (Rose Afriyie & Genevieve Nielsen, Chicago, 2014–present)

Founded after the mayor's innovation team visited Chi Hack Night with a frustration: 10,000 families applied for rental assistance annually; the city could only process 400-600. mRelief simplified eligibility to 10 questions via SMS in 5 minutes. Both founders were personally affected (Afriyie grew up on WIC; Nielsen evacuated from Katrina). Collaborates with 200+ nonprofits, food banks, schools. Connected 1M+ households to SNAP, unlocking ~$1B in benefits.

**Why it worked:** Founded *with* community insight (not imposed on it), founders personally credible, framed as acceleration of human work not replacement.

**Sources:** [Our Story • mRelief](https://www.new.mrelief.com/about-us), [mRelief - Chi Hack Night](https://chihacknight.org/projects/2015/09/01/mrelief)

### Litterati (Jeff Kirschner, 2014–present)

Origin: Kirschner's 4-year-old daughter found a plastic cat-litter tub in the woods. Built a mobile app to crowdsource litter data. Works *with* cities, NGOs, researchers, volunteer cleanup groups. Data fuels advocacy and corporate accountability. The model: Litterati doesn't *own* cleanups — it's infrastructure. Volunteer groups retain control.

**Why it worked:** Founder's personal story made the need real, data (not the app) is the shared asset, volunteer groups own their effort, no claim to own the solution.

**Sources:** [Litterati](https://www.litterati.org/), [Wake County Litterati Partnership](https://www.wake.gov/departments-government/86it-anti-litter-campaign/litterati-partnership/), [Why I started Litterati](https://medium.com/@jeffkirschner/why-i-started-litterati-792d86d0dcc)

### Snap Send Solve (Australia, 2015–present)

Private company; growth driven by *local* government adoption, not venture capital. 850+ Solvers (councils, water authorities, utilities), 4.5M+ reports in 2024. Hume City Council saved 2 minutes of processing per report from day one. Bottom-up adoption: councils see efficiency gains, request integration.

**Why it worked:** No "take it or leave it" mandate, data transparency holds gov accountable, international expansion through local partnerships.

**Sources:** [Snap Send Solve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snap_Send_Solve), [Goulburn-Mulwaree partnership](https://www.goulburn.nsw.gov.au/News-Articles/Goulburn-Mulwaree-Council-Partners-with-Snap-Send-Solve-for-Effortless-Issue-Reporting/)

### Decidim (Barcelona, 2016–present)

Open-source participatory democracy platform launched 2016-01-31. Governance: Decidim Free Software Association (founded 2019) — democratic association. Partnership policy: companies contribute 3% of new service contracts to code maintenance, nonprofits 1.5%, new orgs exempt year one. 450+ organizations in 30+ countries.

**Why it worked:** Born from social movement (not top-down innovation), open-source governance reduces vendor lock-in, nonprofit-friendly pricing, no founder hero narrative.

**Sources:** [Decidim](https://decidim.org/), [Partnership policy](https://decidim.org/partnership-policy/)

## Direct precedent: failed handoffs

### Snapcrap (Sean Miller, San Francisco, 2018)

Miller built a 2-click feces reporter for SF311. October 2018 launch — viral within 24 hours (NBC, LA Times, NY Times, Vice, Joe Rogan podcast). 10,000 downloads in 3 months. **No improvement on the streets.** Feces reports jumped 20,913 → 24,456 (2017→2018 partial) but Snapcrap wasn't integrating with Public Works — it was a wrapper around 311.

**Why it failed:**
1. No partnership conversation with Public Works
2. The real problem was housing/services/sanitation, not app friction
3. No endpoint — no additional crews or budget to handle accelerated intake
4. Founder served by attention, not community

**Key lesson for CleanLA:** A faster reporting app in a broken system is just faster frustration. Confirm the city/partner has capacity to act on what gets reported.

**Sources:** [Snapcrap — Why I built an app](https://medium.com/@miller.stowe/snapcrap-why-i-built-an-app-to-report-poop-on-the-streets-of-san-francisco-aac12382a7ce), [SF's Human Feces Problem — There's an App for That](https://www.govtech.com/civic/snapcrap-creator-provides-visual-tool-to-help-sf-clean-up-its-sidewalks.html)

### Joshua Tauberer's GovTrack-adjacent failures (2005–2013)

GovTrack Insider (real-time congressional reporting), GovTrack Videos, Real Congress (interactive media), ANCFinder (DC hyperlocal tracker), Semantic Web Database, ScrapStats — all failed for the same reason: built "for" the community instead of "with" the community. Information access ≠ engagement. Founder enthusiasm ≠ market demand.

**Source:** [My failures: Civic technology ideas that didn't quite work](https://joshuatauberer.medium.com/my-failures-civic-technology-ideas-that-didn-t-quite-work-c73ecf730032)

### Code for America Brigade sunset (2023)

CfA announced it would "sunset" the 60-chapter Brigade Network. Brigades had no independent legal status, no IP, no capital — when CfA shifted strategy, they had 12 months to reinvent or disappear. SF Civic Tech found refuge under Open Collective Foundation.

**Key lesson:** If a founder or parent org controls IP, brand, and fiscal structure, downstream teams are vulnerable. Successful handoffs require *early* transfer of ownership, not sudden divestment.

**Sources:** [Code for America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_for_America), [Re-Introducing SF Civic Tech](https://www.sfcivictech.org/blog/2024-10-16-re-introducing-sf-civic-tech)

## The first conversation — what works, what doesn't

### Framings that work

- SeeClickFix: "Residents love reporting to this tool; how do we make it official with your systems?"
- mRelief: "The city told us 10,000 families need help but can only process 600. I know this problem personally. Can we solve it together?"
- Litterati: "My daughter found litter in the woods. Volunteers want to track cleanup. Can we help you understand where your litter comes from?"

All three reframe from *founder as solution-provider* to *founder as co-problem-solver*.

### Framings that die

- Snapcrap: "I noticed your feces problem. Here's a faster way to report it." (no validation of bottleneck)
- Tauberer projects: "I built a thing. Want to try it?" (no named user)
- iFollowTheMoney: "We built a platform for civic engagement." (built first, users imagined after)

### The questions that unlock it

Per Laurenellen McCann's community-driven civic tech research: "Ask people what they need, invite them to participate in the design, and evaluate their ability to use technology."

First conversation should be **listening**:
- "What's your biggest bottleneck in cleanup coordination?"
- "If you could change one thing about how volunteers sign up / report damage / track impact, what would it be?"
- "What's a win for you? More volunteers? Better data? Political visibility? Funding proof?"
- "Do you want to *own* this tool, or use a tool I've built?"

That last question is critical. It determines the entire partnership structure.

## Power dynamics — navigating the indie-founder vs. grassroots-leader gap

**Structural imbalance:**

| Founder | Grassroots leader (e.g., Naula) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Often outside the community | Inside the community (identity capital) |
| Built the tool (intellectual capital) | Built relationships and volunteers (social capital) |
| Has tech funding or savings | Usually underfunded |
| Sees problem as technical | Sees problem as systemic |
| Has media-attention option | Focused on immediate impact |

**What kills partnerships:**

1. Founder assumes technical solution *is* the intervention (Snapcrap)
2. Founder retains roadmap control; Juan's needs drift away from what's possible
3. Founder expects gratitude; Juan needs results
4. Publicity misalignment (founder wants the story; Juan wants impact)
5. IP ambiguity creates dependency

**Tactical moves that work:**

1. Come with a sketch, not a finished product — make Juan a co-designer
2. Make Juan the decision-maker on scope
3. Offer multiple IP structures upfront (transfer, license, co-ownership with sunset) — let him pick
4. Keep credit clear and public — Juan and Clean LA With Me built this
5. Define success in Juan's metrics, not yours
6. Plan for your own departure; make yourself obsolete

Ben Berkowitz: "It really comes down to trusting the fact that the people that you're inviting into the process want to help."

## Common deal structures — trade-off matrix

| Structure | IP | Founder role | When to use |
|-----------|-----|--------------|-------------|
| **Outright donation** | Juan owns all | Exits | Founder has other priorities; Juan has in-house tech |
| **Founder employed** | Nonprofit owns | Joins as CTO | Founder mission-driven; Juan has funding to hire |
| **Co-ownership w/ sunset** | Joint for N years, then Juan | Co-steward, planned exit | Both willing to invest 2-3 years |
| **License-only** | Founder retains | Maintains updates | Founder wants to license to multiple orgs |
| **Fiscal sponsorship then spinoff** | Held by sponsor | Project lead | Neither party incorporated; early-stage |

The right structure depends on Juan's answer to "what do you want to own?", not on what the founder prefers.

## Recommended conversation script / agenda (30-60 min)

**Phase 1: Listen (5-10 min).** "Can you walk me through how you currently coordinate a cleanup? How do volunteers sign up? How do you track impact?"

**Phase 2: Understand constraints (5-10 min).** "What's been the hardest part of growing Clean LA With Me?"

**Phase 3: Surface what you built (10 min).** Show a sketch or rough prototype. Let him click it. Ask: "Does this match how you'd want to show impact?"

**Phase 4: Clarify decision-making authority (5 min).** "Who would decide what features get built and in what order?" "Would you want to own the code and data, or just use it?" *Critical question.*

**Phase 5: Address concerns (5 min).** Anticipate: time, sustainability, cost. Have clean answers ready.

**Phase 6: Next steps (2-3 min).** Propose a two-week feedback cycle, three test volunteers, clear success metric.

## Red flags to listen for (Juan saying these = caution)

- "Our volunteers are not tech-savvy" — adoption risk
- "I want you to build this for us, then hand it off" — transactional; verify tech capacity
- "I'll need to run this by the board" — decision isn't his alone
- "Let me think about it" — polite no
- Silence — polite no

## Green flags to listen for

- "This is exactly the problem we've been trying to solve"
- "Can we modify this to also track X?" (ownership thinking)
- "Let's start small and see if it works"
- "Can you train our team?" (adoption commitment)
- "This would help us prove our impact to donors / city / press" (high-leverage use case)
- "I want to own this so we can fork it" (independence)
- "Can I introduce you to other cleanup orgs?" (ecosystem thinking)

## The specific CleanLA scenario: tech-founder + cleanup-org

Limited direct precedent. Litterati is the closest — data-collection infrastructure that *amplifies* cleanup orgs' work without trying to organize them. The pattern:

- **Boots on ground** (Juan's role): can't be replaced
- **Data in the cloud** (CleanLA's role): captures, counts, visualizes impact

The conversation should be: "I can't organize volunteers or do cleanups. But I can give you visibility into impact so you can tell the story better."

---

## Final note

The most consistent signal across all successful partnerships: **the founder solved a real problem first (often personally), then offered it to partners.** Berkowitz had graffiti on his street. Afriyie's family received WIC. Kirschner's daughter found litter.

**For CleanLA:** do you have evidence that *you* use the tool? That you've personally fixed something with it? That Juan and his volunteers have *already asked* for it? Or are you approaching Juan with a finished product hoping he'll adopt it?

The former gets partnerships. The latter gets polite rejection.
